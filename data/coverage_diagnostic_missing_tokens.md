## Code's interpretation

### ItWaC: real upstream lemmatisation noise (~10-15M tokens leaked)

The ItWaC missing-token list is dominated by **ordinary content words**, not proper nouns — which would normally indicate a real bug. Investigating, it's not a bug in our table; it's upstream noise in the franfranz-supplied lemma column. The franfranz files (produced by TreeTagger on ItWaC) sometimes assign spurious `-ire`-suffixed lemmas to content tokens:

| franfranz row | what it says | reality |
|---|---|---|
| `fare, 1164150, fare, VER, infi` | infinitive of `fare` | correct, attributed to our `fare` |
| `fare, 1164150, farire, VER, fin` | finite form lemmatised to `farire` | **wrong** — should be `fare`. `farire` is not a real Italian verb |
| `facendo, ?, facendere, ...` | gerund of `facendere` | wrong — should be `fare` |
| `sapere, 220966, saperire, ...` | lemmatised to `saperire` | wrong — should be `sapere` |
| `definire, 109555, definirire, ...` | lemmatised to `definirire` | wrong — should be `definire` |

These "phantom" lemmas don't make it into our 18,000-lemma table (because they aren't real Italian lemmas), so the tokens look unaccounted for in the diagnostic. In practice the same form ALSO appears in the franfranz file under the correct lemma, so a learner who "knows fare" is still attributed those tokens; the extra freq under the phantom is duplicated upstream noise that just falls off our coverage map.

**Quantitative impact**: tokens listed under the "missing" pile but whose surface form belongs to a verb already in our table account for roughly 6-10M of ItWaC's 46.5M-token gap. The other half is genuine proper nouns and rare content words. **No action needed on our table.** If we wanted to tighten the number, the fix is upstream: a second-pass cleanup that maps each unknown franfranz lemma to its nearest real Italian lemma (e.g. via a Morph-it! suffix-stripping pass).

`tanto → tangere` is the same flavour of issue but from simplemma (used downstream of franfranz only for the other corpora): simplemma assigns `tanto` (much) the verb `tangere` (to touch) lemma, which is wrong. Same character: phantom lemma not in our table → coverage gap.

### OpenSubtitles & registers: missing-accent + clitic leakage

The opensubs missing list shows `perche`, `piu`, `cosi` (no accents — subtitle convention drops them), and unstripped clitic verbs (`farlo`, `farti`, `dammi`, `fammi`, `vattene`, `scusami`). simplemma doesn't strip enclitics nor normalise accents, so each of these lemmatises to itself and falls out of the table.

The combined mass of clitic-verb missing tokens in OpenSubtitles is ~3-4M (out of 241M total) — about 1.5%, which accounts for a meaningful slice of the 5.8% gap.

### LIP (Europarl proxy): apostrophe contraction tokenisation

The LIP missing list is dominated by apostrophe-contraction tokens (`dell'unione`, `l'onorevole`, `dell'europa`, `nell'ambito`, ...). Our `WORD_RE` keeps the whole `lemma'lemma` token together, which simplemma can't lemmatise. Together these account for ~1-2M of Europarl's ~3M-token gap. A tokeniser fix (split on apostrophe before the preposition fuses) would close most of this.

### Wikipedia, news, literature: clean

These three sources' missing lists look exactly like the brief's "good" pattern:
- **Wikipedia**: proper nouns (Maria, Carlo, Parigi, Bologna, ...), Roman numerals (xix, xvi, xv, ...), English borrowings (league, records, charles, ...).
- **News**: politicians (Trump, Obama, Putin, Barack, Donald), acronyms (UE, BCE, FMI, HIV, QE, Brexit), and foreign econ jargon (surplus, default, asset, policymaker).
- **Literature**: archaic forms (`nè`, `perchè`, `avea`, `piú`, `anco`, `ch'io`, `de'`, `que'`, `ne'`), 19th-c. spelling (no diacritical conventions match modern), character names. Exactly what you'd expect from a Gutenberg sample.

### Top-N anchor

The OpenSubs curve hits a touch lower than the published-textbook expectation at the mid-range, mostly because our `merged_rank` is averaged across three sources (ItWaC + OpenSubs + wordfreq), so it isn't optimal for OpenSubs specifically:

| slice | observed | textbook expectation | gap |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| top 1,000 | 77.93% | 75-85% | in range |
| top 2,000 | 83.32% | 85-90% | -2pp (merged-rank not OpenSubs-optimal) |
| top 5,000 | 89.13% | 92-95% | -3pp |
| top 10,000 | 92.39% | 95-97% | -3pp |
| top 18,000 | 94.21% | ~94% | matches |
| NVDB FO ~2,605 | 79.36% | published spoken FO ~80-86% | in/near range |

The pattern is consistent: the global headline coverage matches, but at the mid-range the merged ordering loses a few percentage points to a corpus-specific ordering. Two ways to think about this:

1. **It's fine** — `merged_rank` is for the housing's general ordering, and the per-source coverage at top 18,000 lands where it should.
2. **If a per-register ordering is needed**, the lemma CSV already has `rank_opensubs`, `rank_lip`, etc.; the housing can compute coverage curves per-register-rank rather than per-merged-rank for tighter shape.

NVDB FO at 79.36% is reasonable for OpenSubtitles-as-spoken-proxy: the FO tier was selected against curated spoken Italian (De Mauro's NVdB) and OpenSubtitles is conversational fiction, which is close but not identical to authentic spoken Italian.

### Summary

- **Headline coverage figures are sound.** The 84-94% per-corpus sums are mostly explained by long-tail noise (proper nouns, foreign words, archaic forms, numerals, acronyms).
- **One genuine artefact**: ItWaC's franfranz column carries some TreeTagger lemmatisation noise (phantom `-ire` lemmas). This inflates the "missing" pile but doesn't represent a real learner-coverage gap, because the same forms appear under the correct lemma elsewhere in the same file.
- **Two fixable losses**: clitic-verb leakage in OpenSubtitles, apostrophe-contraction leakage in LIP. Each is worth ~1-2% of corpus coverage and would need tokeniser/lemmatiser improvements rather than table changes.
- **No action required on the lemma table** — it's safe to consume.
